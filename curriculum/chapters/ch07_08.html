Serial Correlation and Detrending

Chapters 7-8: Why standard errors lie, and what happens when you strip out trends

7 The Independence Assumption and Serial Correlation

What you learn OLS assumes that errors are independent: \(\text{cor}(e_i, e_j) = 0\) for \(i \neq j\). In panel data, this fails. A country with high obesity in 2015 has high obesity in 2016. The TWFE residuals in our data have a mean AR(1) of 0.90 -- consecutive residuals are almost as correlated as the original series. Non-clustered standard errors pretend each observation is a new piece of information. They are not.

The assumption hierarchy

STAT 538 teaches the four regression assumptions in order of severity. Linearity comes first (if the model is wrong, nothing else matters). Then independence. Then constant variance. Then normality. Serial correlation sits second in this hierarchy -- above heteroskedasticity -- because it makes the data carry less information than the sample size suggests.

When errors are serially correlated, OLS estimates are still unbiased (the coefficients are right on average), but the standard errors are wrong. Specifically, they are too small. The model thinks it has 481 independent observations, but within each country, 13 years of data behave more like a few independent draws.

The math For an AR(1) process with autocorrelation \(\rho\), the effective sample size within a group of \(T\) observations is approximately: $$n_{\text{eff}} \approx T \cdot \frac{1 - \rho}{1 + \rho}$$ With \(\rho = 0.90\) and \(T = 13\) years, each country contributes roughly \(13 \times \frac{0.10}{1.90} \approx 0.7\) effective observations. Thirty-seven countries times 0.7 gives about 26 effective observations -- not 481.

Why this matters for published results

A coefficient that appears significant at \(t = 2.0\) with non-clustered errors would require \(t = 5\) to \(6\) to survive clustering. Bertrand, Duflo, and Mullainathan (2004) demonstrated this problem two decades ago for differences-in-differences designs. It applies with equal force to country-level food supply panels.

In the Sugar Paradox, SE inflation does not explain the null -- the coefficient itself is near zero. But it explains how weak signals can appear significant in published studies that use non-clustered standard errors.

Deep dive: Durbin-Watson and AR(1) detection

The classical test for serial correlation is the Durbin-Watson statistic:

$$DW = \frac{\sum_{t=2}^{T}(\hat{e}_t - \hat{e}_{t-1})^2}{\sum_{t=1}^{T}\hat{e}_t^2}$$

DW ranges from 0 to 4. Under no serial correlation, \(DW \approx 2\). With positive autocorrelation (our case), \(DW \ll 2\) because consecutive residuals are similar, making \((\hat{e}_t - \hat{e}_{t-1})^2\) small. There is a direct relationship: \(DW \approx 2(1 - \hat{\rho})\), so \(\hat{\rho} = 0.90\) implies \(DW \approx 0.20\).

An alternative is the lag-1 scatterplot: plot \(\hat{e}_{t+1}\) against \(\hat{e}_t\). A strong positive trend in this plot (as in our data) confirms AR(1) structure. The slope of the regression through the origin gives \(\hat{\rho}\) directly.

Quiz yourself

If the mean AR(1) is 0.90 and there are 13 years per country, why can't we just multiply the standard errors by some fixed correction factor?
Because the correction depends on the autocorrelation structure, which varies across countries (range: 0.11 to 1.00 in this data) and may not be pure AR(1). Clustered standard errors handle this automatically by treating each country as a single cluster, allowing arbitrary within-cluster correlation. No parametric assumption about the form of dependence is needed.
The paper says the non-clustered one-way FE t-statistic for sugar is 0.66, and the clustered t is 0.25. Both are non-significant. So why does clustering matter here?
In THIS case, the coefficient is null either way. But the paper names Lin et al. (2018) as finding sugar significant in a global panel. If their t-statistics were in the range of 2.0 to 3.0 without clustering, they would fall to 0.7 to 1.1 with clustering -- no longer significant. The SE inflation factor of 2.6-2.9x is the mechanism by which inadequate clustering can turn nulls into "findings."
Which model assumption does serial correlation violate: linearity, independence, constant variance, or normality?
Independence. The assumption \(\text{cor}(e_i, e_j) = 0\) for \(i \neq j\) fails because consecutive observations within the same country are positively correlated. In STAT 538's assumption hierarchy, this is the second most serious violation (after linearity), because it means the data contain less information than the sample size suggests.

Serial correlation inflates standard errors but does not change the coefficient. The sugar coefficient itself is near zero. The next chapter asks a different question: what if the problem is not with the standard errors but with the levels? When both sugar and obesity are trending upward, their correlation could be entirely spurious. Detrending and first differences strip out the trends and see what remains.

8 Detrending and First Differences

What you learn STAT 538 Module 3 teaches transformations that fix assumption violations. Detrending and first-differencing are transformations that fix a specific kind of confounding: shared time trends. When two variables both trend upward, they will be correlated even if they are causally unrelated. Removing the trend is a transformation on the data before fitting, just like a log transform or Box-Cox.

Three ways to strip out trends

Detrending: For each country, fit a linear trend to obesity, sugar, and oil separately. Subtract the fitted trend. What remains are the deviations from each country's trajectory. If sugar causes obesity, countries that deviate upward from their sugar trend should deviate upward from their obesity trend.

First differences (FD): Replace each variable with its year-to-year change: \(\Delta y_t = y_t - y_{t-1}\). This removes country-specific levels AND any linear trend in a single operation. It asks: in years when a country's sugar supply increased, did its obesity also increase more than usual?

Long differences (LD): Compare only the endpoints: the 2010 value and the 2020 value for each country. This gives one observation per country (N = 37) and asks: did countries whose sugar supply changed more also see more obesity change?

The math of detrending For country \(i\) with observations \(t = 1, \ldots, T\), fit a linear trend: $$\hat{y}_{it} = \hat{\alpha}_i + \hat{\delta}_i \cdot t$$ The detrended variable is the residual: \(\tilde{y}_{it} = y_{it} - \hat{y}_{it}\). Apply the same procedure to sugar and oil. Then regress detrended obesity on detrended sugar. The slope answers: within a country, after removing its trajectory, does deviation in sugar predict deviation in obesity?

The results

All three approaches yield the same answer: nothing.

# Detrend: remove country-specific linear trends
for iso3 in women["iso3"].unique():
    mask = women["iso3"] == iso3
    for col in ["obesity", "sugar_10kcal", "oil_100kcal"]:
        vals = women.loc[mask, col].values
        t = np.arange(len(vals), dtype=float)
        slope, intercept = np.polyfit(t, vals, 1)
        women.loc[mask, col] = vals - (slope * t + intercept)

fit_dt = smf.ols("obesity ~ sugar_10kcal + oil_100kcal", data=women_dt
).fit(cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": women_dt["iso3"]})
Detrended: β = -0.001   t = -0.13   p = 0.90
First differences: r = -0.03   p = 0.59   (N = 444)
Long differences: r = -0.19   p = 0.25   (N = 37)

The detrended regression yields \(t = -0.13\). First differences give \(r = -0.03\). Long differences give \(r = -0.19\). All three are dead null. The correlation visible in levels was entirely driven by shared upward trends -- richer, more urbanized countries have more sugar AND more obesity, and both variables trend upward over time.

Paper connection Table 1, row 4: Detrended, \(\hat{\beta} = -0.001\), \(t = -0.13\), \(p = 0.90\). Section 3.2: First differences \(r = -0.03\) (\(p = 0.59\), \(N = 444\)). Long differences \(r = -0.19\) (\(p = 0.25\), \(N = 37\)). "Because long differences do not depend on year-to-year variation, they resist the NCD-RisC smoothing."

Why long differences matter

One objection to the null result is that NCD-RisC obesity estimates are smoothed by a Bayesian hierarchical model, which compresses year-to-year variation. First differences and TWFE rely on that year-to-year variation, so they might miss a real signal that the smoothing suppressed.

Long differences sidestep this objection entirely. They use only the 2010 and 2020 endpoints -- no year-to-year variation needed. If sugar supply had a real within-country effect large enough to matter, a 10-year change in sugar supply should predict a 10-year change in obesity. It does not (\(r = -0.19\), \(p = 0.25\)).

Deep dive: Detrending vs. TWFE vs. first differences

These three approaches remove trends differently:

TWFE removes a common time effect shared by all countries (year dummies). It does NOT remove country-specific time trends. If Senegal's obesity grows faster than average, TWFE does not account for that.

Detrending removes each country's individual linear trajectory. This is more aggressive than TWFE: it removes both the common trend and country-specific slopes.

First differences are algebraically equivalent to including country-specific linear trends in a panel regression. By replacing levels with changes, they remove any deterministic trend (linear or otherwise) in one step.

The fact that all three give the same null answer is powerful. If the null depended on the specific deconfounding strategy, we would worry. But TWFE (\(t = -0.64\)), entity-specific detrending (\(t = -0.13\)), and first differences (\(r = -0.03\)) all converge. The null is robust to the choice of trend-removal method.

Deep dive: Connection to STAT 538 Module 3 (Transformations)

Module 3 teaches transformations to fix assumption violations: log transforms for right-skewed residuals, Box-Cox for choosing the optimal power transformation, variance-stabilizing transforms for heteroskedasticity. Detrending and first-differencing are in the same family -- they are transformations of the data applied before fitting, designed to fix a specific problem (spurious correlation from shared trends).

The Box-Cox approach asks: "What power transformation of Y makes the residuals best satisfy assumptions?" Detrending asks: "What linear function of time, subtracted from Y and X, removes the spurious trend component?" Both are pre-fit transformations with the same goal: make the data suitable for OLS inference.

Quiz yourself

Why does R-squared drop from 99.4% (TWFE) to about 1% (detrended)?
TWFE's high R-squared comes mostly from the country and year dummies, which absorb levels and shared trends. After detrending, those patterns are already removed from the data. The detrended regression has no dummies -- it regresses residual-from-trend obesity on residual-from-trend sugar. With the trends gone, there is almost no structure left to explain. The 1% R-squared reflects how little detrended sugar predicts detrended obesity.
The long-difference r = -0.19 is the largest in magnitude of the three. Could this be a real (negative) signal?
It is the largest, but with only N = 37 countries, \(p = 0.25\) -- far from significant. The 95% confidence interval for r includes zero. Moreover, the negative sign (countries that increased sugar supply saw slightly LESS obesity growth) would contradict the positive cross-sectional relationship. The most parsimonious explanation is noise in a small sample.
A colleague argues: "Detrending throws away the real variation. Of course the result is null -- you removed the signal." How do you respond?
This argument confuses between-country variation (levels) with within-country variation (changes). The cross-sectional "signal" comes from the fact that countries with more sugar are fatter. But that could be confounding -- those same countries are richer, more urbanized, and further along the nutrition transition. The causal question is whether changes in sugar supply within a country predict changes in obesity. Detrending isolates exactly that variation. If sugar caused obesity, detrended sugar should predict detrended obesity. It does not.

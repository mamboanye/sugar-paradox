Chapters 9-10: Mundlak decomposition and simulation-based inference

9 The Mundlak/CRE Decomposition

What you learn The Correlated Random Effects (CRE) model, also called the Mundlak device, includes both the time-varying predictor and its country mean as separate regressors. The coefficient on the time-varying part estimates the within-country association. The coefficient on the mean estimates the between-country association. This cleanly separates the two sources of variation that pooled OLS confounds.

The question we need answered

Chapters 5-8 showed that the sugar-obesity association disappears once we control for country and year effects. But those models only tell us the within-country estimate is null. They do not show us, side by side, how much of the original signal was between-country versus within-country. The Mundlak decomposition does.

How it works

For each country \(i\) and year \(t\), decompose sugar supply into two pieces:

\(\text{sugar}_{it} = \underbrace{\bar{\text{sugar}}_i}_{\text{between}} + \underbrace{(\text{sugar}_{it} - \bar{\text{sugar}}_i)}_{\text{within}}\)

The country mean \(\bar{\text{sugar}}_i\) captures cross-sectional differences: Mauritius has high sugar supply, Niger has low. The deviation \((\text{sugar}_{it} - \bar{\text{sugar}}_i)\) captures temporal changes within each country: did Nigeria's sugar supply go up or down relative to its own average?

Include both in a single regression with year fixed effects and cluster standard errors by country:

$$\text{obesity}_{it} = \beta_W (\text{sugar}_{it} - \bar{\text{sugar}}_i) + \beta_B \bar{\text{sugar}}_i + \gamma_W (\text{oil}_{it} - \bar{\text{oil}}_i) + \gamma_B \bar{\text{oil}}_i + \delta_t + \varepsilon_{it}$$

\(\beta_W\) is the within-country effect. \(\beta_B\) is the between-country effect. If sugar genuinely causes obesity, both should be positive and significant. If the cross-sectional association is driven by confounding (richer, more urbanized countries have both more sugar and more obesity), then \(\beta_B\) will be large and \(\beta_W\) will be near zero.

The code

# Mundlak/CRE decomposition
women_cre = women.copy()
for col in ["sugar_10kcal", "oil_100kcal"]:
    means = women_cre.groupby("iso3")[col].transform("mean")
    women_cre[f"{col}_between"] = means
    women_cre[f"{col}_within"]  = women_cre[col] - means

fit_cre = smf.ols(
    "obesity_prevalence_pct ~ sugar_10kcal_within + sugar_10kcal_between"
    " + oil_100kcal_within + oil_100kcal_between + C(year)",
    data=women_cre,
).fit(cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": women_cre["iso3"]})

print(f"Within:  beta = {fit_cre.params['sugar_10kcal_within']:.4f}, "
      f"t = {fit_cre.tvalues['sugar_10kcal_within']:.2f}")
print(f"Between: beta = {fit_cre.params['sugar_10kcal_between']:.4f}, "
      f"t = {fit_cre.tvalues['sugar_10kcal_between']:.2f}")

Between: beta = 0.6157, t = 3.46

Why this matters for causal inference

Between-country variation is confounded by everything that differs between rich and poor countries: food system infrastructure, urbanization history, healthcare access, built environment, employment patterns. When \(\beta_B\) is large and \(\beta_W\) is null, the parsimonious explanation is that sugar supply is a marker of development, not a cause of obesity.

The within-country estimate strips out all time-invariant confounders (just like country fixed effects). In fact, the CRE within-country coefficient is numerically identical to the fixed effects estimate. The Mundlak device does not produce new information about the within-country relationship. What it adds is the explicit between-country coefficient, making the decomposition visible.

Deep dive: Why is CRE equivalent to fixed effects?

The Mundlak (1978) result: in a panel model, adding the group means of all time-varying regressors as additional controls produces the same within-group estimates as including group fixed effects. Formally, the CRE model

\(\text{obesity}_{it} = \beta_W \tilde{x}_{it} + \beta_B \bar{x}_i + \delta_t + \varepsilon_{it}\)

yields the same \(\hat\beta_W\) as

\(\text{obesity}_{it} = \beta x_{it} + \alpha_i + \delta_t + \varepsilon_{it}\)

The algebraic reason: including \(\bar{x}_i\) as a regressor absorbs the same variation as the country dummy \(\alpha_i\), because the country dummy is a linear function of all the country means. The CRE just reparameterizes the fixed effects model to make \(\beta_B\) separately estimable.

This equivalence holds exactly in balanced panels and approximately in unbalanced ones (as here, where missingness is minor). In the sugar data, the CRE within-country \(t = -0.66\) matches the TWFE \(t = -0.64\) closely. The small difference arises because the CRE includes year effects but not explicit country dummies, so the degrees of freedom differ slightly.

Deep dive: The Hausman test connection

The Hausman test compares fixed effects to random effects by testing whether the within-country and between-country coefficients are equal: \(H_0: \beta_W = \beta_B\). If they differ, the between-country estimate is biased by confounders and you should use fixed effects (or equivalently, trust only the within-country estimate).

In the CRE framework, this test is equivalent to testing whether the coefficient on the country mean is significant after controlling for the within-country deviation. Here, \(\beta_B = 0.616\) and \(\beta_W = -0.029\) are dramatically different. A Hausman test would reject equality, confirming that the between-country association is contaminated by confounders and that the within-country estimate (the null) is the appropriate one.

Paper connection Section 3.2: "correlated random effects" is one of six within-country specifications that yield a null. The within \(t = -0.66\) and between \(t = 3.46\) appear in the paper's specification cascade. The CRE is listed alongside fixed effects, TWFE, detrending, first differences, and long differences as converging on the same answer.

Quiz yourself

If the CRE within-country estimate is numerically (almost) the same as the fixed effects estimate, why bother with the CRE at all?
Because the CRE makes the between-country coefficient explicitly visible. Fixed effects absorb the between-country variation into the country dummies, which you typically do not examine. The CRE reparameterizes the model so you can see both coefficients side by side and directly compare them. This makes the decomposition -- all the signal is between-country, none is within-country -- immediately legible.
In the STAT 538 permutation test framework (M5-V2), you permute the response to break the relationship with all predictors, or permute one predictor to test just that variable. How does the Mundlak decomposition relate to the permutation logic?
The Mundlak decomposition separates two kinds of variation that a permutation test would scramble together. Permuting sugar across all observations destroys both within-country and between-country variation. The Mundlak model lets you see that the between-country variation is the only source of the pooled signal. A permutation test on the within-country deviations alone (shuffling sugar within each country, as Chapter 10 does) tests exactly the within-country component that the CRE estimates.
The M5 problem set asks about the F-test for dropping a group of predictors using anova(). If you ran anova() to test whether the between-country sugar coefficient equals the within-country sugar coefficient, what would you expect?
You would reject equality. The between-country coefficient (0.616, t = 3.46) and within-country coefficient (-0.029, t = -0.66) are dramatically different. This is the Hausman test in disguise: when between and within coefficients diverge, confounders are driving the between-country association. The F-test comparing the restricted model (one pooled sugar coefficient) to the unrestricted model (separate within and between coefficients) would be significant, telling you that pooling is inappropriate.

The Mundlak decomposition confirms the story: all signal is between countries, none is within. But can we trust the standard errors? With only 37 clusters and strong serial correlation, asymptotic inference may be unreliable. Chapter 10 builds the null distribution from scratch, using simulation.

10 Simulation: Bootstrap and Permutation

What you learn When you do not trust the asymptotic distribution (here: only 37 clusters, strong serial correlation, residual AR(1) of 0.90-0.98), you can build the null distribution yourself. The permutation test asks: "If sugar had no within-country relationship with obesity, what would the t-statistic look like?" The wild cluster bootstrap asks: "How extreme is my observed t under the null?" Both confirm: \(t = -0.64\) is not unusual.

Two simulation strategies

STAT 538 Module 5 introduces two simulation-based alternatives to parametric inference:

Permutation test. Scramble the predictor to destroy any real relationship, refit the model, record the test statistic. Repeat many times. The fraction of simulated statistics as extreme as the observed one is the p-value. No distributional assumptions required.

Bootstrap. Resample the data (or the residuals) to build a distribution of the test statistic under sampling variability. The M5 lecture covers residual bootstrap (resample residuals, add to fitted values) and pairs bootstrap (resample whole rows). For clustered panel data, the wild cluster bootstrap is more appropriate: it resamples at the cluster level by flipping the sign of entire countries' residuals.

The permutation test

The idea: if sugar has no within-country relationship with obesity, then the temporal ordering of sugar values within each country is arbitrary. Shuffling the years within each country should produce t-statistics that look just like the real one.

The procedure: for each of 999 iterations, shuffle sugar values within each country (preserving the country-level structure but breaking the temporal link), refit the TWFE model with clustered standard errors, and store the t-statistic. The observed \(t = -0.64\) is compared to this null distribution.

The wild cluster bootstrap

The permutation test destroys the within-country relationship by shuffling. The wild cluster bootstrap takes a different approach: it imposes the null by construction and asks how often random noise alone produces a t-statistic as large as the observed one.

The procedure uses Rademacher weights: for each of 999 iterations, assign each country a random weight of +1 or -1. Multiply each country's residuals by its weight, add to fitted values to create a synthetic outcome, refit the model, and record the t-statistic. The fraction of bootstrap t-statistics with \(|t^*| \geq |t_{\text{obs}}|\) is the bootstrap p-value.

# Wild cluster bootstrap with Rademacher weights
fit = smf.ols(formula, data=women).fit(
    cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": women["iso3"]})
observed_t = fit.params["sugar_10kcal"] / fit.bse["sugar_10kcal"]

clusters = women["iso3"].unique()
boot_ts = []
for _ in range(999):
    # Flip entire countries: +1 or -1
    weights = rng.choice([-1, 1], size=len(clusters))
    weight_map = dict(zip(clusters, weights))
    cluster_weights = women["iso3"].map(weight_map)
    # Synthetic Y = fitted + (residual * random sign)
    y_boot = fit.fittedvalues + fit.resid * cluster_weights
    # Refit and record t
    fit_b = smf.ols(formula_boot, data=data_boot).fit(
        cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": women["iso3"]})
    boot_ts.append(fit_b.params["sugar_10kcal"] / fit_b.bse["sugar_10kcal"])

boot_p = np.mean(np.abs(boot_ts) >= np.abs(observed_t))
One-way FE bootstrap p = 0.80    TWFE bootstrap p = 0.58
Deep dive: Why Rademacher weights instead of resampling rows?

The M5 lecture introduces the pairs bootstrap (resample entire rows) and the residual bootstrap (resample residuals). For clustered panel data, neither is ideal. Resampling rows ignores the within-cluster correlation. Resampling residuals ignores the cluster structure.

The wild cluster bootstrap (Cameron, Gelbach, and Miller, 2008) respects both by keeping the cluster structure intact and only perturbing the sign of each cluster's residual block. With Rademacher weights (+1 or -1 with equal probability), the bootstrap DGP has the same first two moments as the original under the null. This is the standard approach for inference with few clusters.

Deep dive: Restricted vs. unrestricted residuals

A technical subtlety: the bootstrap above uses residuals from the unrestricted model (the model that includes sugar as a regressor). The "correct" wild cluster bootstrap for testing \(H_0: \beta_{\text{sugar}} = 0\) should ideally use residuals from the restricted model (the model without sugar), because the null hypothesis says sugar does not belong. Using unrestricted residuals is an approximation that is common in practice and performs well when the coefficient being tested is small, as it is here (\(\beta = -0.029\)). With a t-statistic of -0.64, the restricted and unrestricted residuals are nearly identical, so the approximation is excellent.

Connecting to M5: permuting one predictor

The M5 lecture shows how to permute a single predictor to test that specific variable, using the Galapagos islands example:

# M5 pattern: permute one predictor
tstats = numeric(nreps)
for(i in 1:nreps){
  lmods = lm(Species ~ Nearest + sample(Scruz), gala)
  tstats[i] = summary(lmods)$coef[3,3]
}
mean(abs(tstats) > abs(observed_t))  # two-sided p-value

The sugar permutation test does the same thing, but with a panel twist: it shuffles sugar values within each country rather than across the entire dataset. This preserves the country-level structure (each country keeps its own set of sugar values, just in random order) while destroying only the within-country temporal relationship. This is the correct permutation scheme for panel data with fixed effects.

# Panel permutation: shuffle within each country
perm_ts = []
for _ in range(999):
    w_perm = women.copy()
    for iso3 in w_perm["iso3"].unique():
        mask = w_perm["iso3"] == iso3
        vals = w_perm.loc[mask, "sugar_10kcal"].values.copy()
        rng.shuffle(vals)  # shuffle within country only
        w_perm.loc[mask, "sugar_10kcal"] = vals
    fit_p = smf.ols(
        "obesity_prevalence_pct ~ sugar_10kcal + oil_100kcal + C(iso3) + C(year)",
        data=w_perm
    ).fit(cov_type="cluster", cov_kwds={"groups": w_perm["iso3"]})
    perm_ts.append(fit_p.params["sugar_10kcal"] / fit_p.bse["sugar_10kcal"])

perm_p = np.mean(np.abs(perm_ts) >= np.abs(observed_t))
Observed t = -0.64    Permutation p = 0.58

What the M5 Problem Set teaches about this

Question 4 of the M5 Problem Set asks how to modify a loop to compute a permutation test for the overall F-statistic. The answer: wrap the response variable in sample(). This breaks all relationships simultaneously. The sugar permutation test is the single-predictor variant: wrap just the sugar column in the equivalent of sample(), but do it within each country to respect the panel structure.

Question 5 asks about pairs bootstrap confidence intervals and which coefficient's upper endpoint changes most. The key insight: when bootstrap intervals diverge from parametric intervals, the parametric assumptions are violated. In the sugar data, the wild cluster bootstrap p-values (0.80 for one-way FE, 0.58 for TWFE) are consistent with the clustered asymptotic p-values (0.80 and 0.52), confirming that the clustered standard errors are appropriate despite having only 37 clusters.

Paper connection Section 3.2: "Wild-cluster bootstrap p-values match the asymptotic clustered results: p = 0.80 for country fixed effects and p = 0.58 for two-way fixed effects (999 bootstrap draws). A permutation test that shuffles within-country sugar ordering yields p = 0.58. Two-way clustering by country and year gives t = -0.63, identical to one-way clustering. The null does not depend on the choice of standard error."

Quiz yourself

Why does the permutation test shuffle sugar within each country rather than across the entire dataset?
Because the TWFE model includes country fixed effects. Shuffling across countries would mix between-country variation into the null distribution, producing artificially large t-statistics under the null. Shuffling within countries preserves the cross-sectional structure and tests only the within-country temporal relationship, which is what the TWFE coefficient measures.
Bootstrap p = 0.80 for one-way FE but 0.58 for TWFE. Why is the one-way FE bootstrap p-value higher?
The one-way FE model has larger residual variance (no year effects absorb the shared trend). This means the null distribution of t-statistics is wider, so the observed t is even less unusual. Adding year effects tightens the residuals and narrows the null distribution, but the observed t is still well within it. Both specifications confirm the null; the one-way FE just confirms it more emphatically.
The M5 lecture distinguishes residual bootstrap (fixed X) from pairs bootstrap (random X). Which is more appropriate for the sugar panel data, and why?
Neither is ideal. The pairs bootstrap treats X as random, which is closer to the truth for observational data (countries are "sampled" from a population). But resampling entire rows ignores within-cluster correlation. The wild cluster bootstrap is a better choice: it respects the cluster structure by resampling at the country level (flipping entire countries' residuals) rather than at the observation level. This is the panel-data adaptation of the residual bootstrap.
If the permutation p-value were 0.04 instead of 0.58, but the asymptotic clustered p-value were 0.52, what would that imply?
It would imply that the asymptotic clustered standard errors are too conservative -- inflating the standard error beyond what the data support. This could happen if the clustering correction overcorrects for serial correlation. In practice, the opposite concern is more common (asymptotic SEs too liberal with few clusters). The fact that both methods agree here is reassuring: the null is real, not an artifact of the inference method.
Deep dive: How many permutations are enough?

The sugar analysis uses 999 permutations. Is that enough? The precision of a permutation p-value with \(B\) permutations is approximately \(\pm 1/\sqrt{B}\). With \(B = 999\), the standard error of the p-value estimate is about 0.03. Since the observed p = 0.58 is far from any conventional threshold (0.05, 0.01), 999 iterations provide more than sufficient precision. If the p-value were near 0.05, you would want 9,999 or more permutations to distinguish a borderline case.

Chapters 9 and 10 close every inferential objection. The Mundlak decomposition shows the signal is entirely between-country. The permutation and bootstrap tests show the within-country null is not an artifact of standard error choice or distributional assumptions.

1 Vectors and Correlation STAT 538 M1

Core idea Each column of data is a vector. The correlation between two variables is the cosine of the angle between their centered vectors. When we say \(r = 0.677\), we are saying the angle between the centered sugar and obesity vectors is about 47 degrees.

Data as vectors

We have 37 Sub-Saharan African countries. For each country, we measure two things: sugar supply (kcal per capita per day) and women's obesity prevalence (%). Average both over 2018-2020 to get a single snapshot per country.

Now think of these measurements as vectors in 37-dimensional space. The sugar vector \(\mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_{37})\) has one entry per country. The obesity vector \(\mathbf{y} = (y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_{37})\) does the same. Each vector is a point in \(\mathbb{R}^{37}\).

Centering

Before computing correlation, we center both vectors by subtracting their means:

$$\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{x} - \bar{x}\mathbf{1}, \qquad \tilde{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{y} - \bar{y}\mathbf{1}$$

Centering moves the origin to the mean of the data, so the vectors now represent deviations from the average. This is what makes correlation a measure of linear association rather than agreement in raw levels.

Correlation is cosine

The Pearson correlation coefficient is defined as:

$$r = \frac{\tilde{\mathbf{x}} \cdot \tilde{\mathbf{y}}}{\|\tilde{\mathbf{x}}\| \; \|\tilde{\mathbf{y}}\|} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2} \; \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \bar{y})^2}} = \cos(\theta)$$

This is exactly the formula for the cosine of the angle between two vectors. When \(r = 1\), the centered vectors point in the same direction (\(\theta = 0^\circ\)). When \(r = 0\), they are perpendicular (\(\theta = 90^\circ\)). When \(r = -1\), they point in opposite directions (\(\theta = 180^\circ\)).

The paper number For our 37 countries, \(r = 0.677\). That means the angle between centered sugar and centered obesity is \(\theta = \arccos(0.677) \approx 47.4^\circ\). The vectors are pointing in roughly the same direction, but they are far from aligned.

Computing it step by step

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

# 37 countries: sugar supply and obesity prevalence (2018-2020 means)
sugar = np.array([129.8, 66.1, 266.0, 104.5, 107.8, 31.4, 114.9,
                  170.7, 175.0, 77.8, 172.7, 125.7, 141.6, 514.3,
                  51.1, 201.9, 68.4, 128.7, 84.5, 112.9, 386.8,
                  341.3, 68.5, 268.2, 54.6, 95.0, 97.7, 165.6,
                  75.6, 163.9, 343.2, 299.4, 112.6, 129.5, 315.9,
                  122.2, 177.6])

obesity = np.array([15.51, 7.99, 26.66, 14.65, 19.66, 8.18, 12.82,
                    22.78, 21.36, 3.88, 30.09, 19.60, 11.36, 18.33,
                    13.77, 17.18, 19.03, 31.65, 3.65, 12.41, 32.88,
                    24.76, 10.52, 22.73, 7.06, 15.38, 6.55, 14.93,
                    10.98, 21.96, 42.86, 39.29, 16.54, 9.99, 45.62,
                    14.88, 19.99])

# Step 1: Center both vectors
x_centered = sugar - sugar.mean()
y_centered = obesity - obesity.mean()

# Step 2: Dot product of centered vectors
dot = np.dot(x_centered, y_centered)

# Step 3: Divide by product of lengths
r = dot / (np.linalg.norm(x_centered) * np.linalg.norm(y_centered))

# Step 4: Verify with scipy
r_scipy, p_scipy = stats.pearsonr(sugar, obesity)

# Step 5: Compute the angle
angle_deg = np.degrees(np.arccos(r))

print(f"Manual r  = {r:.3f}")
print(f"Scipy r   = {r_scipy:.3f}  (p = {p_scipy:.6f})")
print(f"Angle     = {angle_deg:.1f} degrees")
Manual r = 0.677
Scipy r = 0.677 (p = 0.000004)
Angle = 47.4 degrees

This is the number that makes sugar look like it drives obesity -- countries with more sugar supply have more obesity. Every chapter from here forward will interrogate this number.

Why cosine?

The dot product formula \(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \|\mathbf{a}\| \|\mathbf{b}\| \cos\theta\) is a fact of linear algebra that holds in any number of dimensions. When you divide the dot product by the two vector lengths, you isolate the angle -- the direction of association stripped of scale. That is exactly what correlation does: it removes scale (via centering and normalizing by the standard deviations) and measures only the directional alignment between two variables.

The connection is not just an analogy. It is literally the same operation. The Pearson \(r\) is the cosine of the angle between centered data vectors. This geometric fact will matter in Chapter 2, where regression becomes projection and \(R^2\) becomes \(\cos^2\) of a different angle.

Check your understanding

In simple linear regression, the explanatory variable x differs from the response y in what way?
The value of \(x\) can sometimes be chosen by the person designing the study or experiment. In our data, nobody chose sugar supply levels -- they are observational -- but the asymmetry remains: we are asking whether sugar (x) predicts obesity (y), not the reverse. (Adapted from STAT 538 M1 Reading Quiz Q1.)
If the angle between centered sugar and centered obesity were 90 degrees, what would r be? What would that mean?
\(\cos(90^\circ) = 0\), so \(r = 0\). The two variables would be linearly uncorrelated -- knowing a country's sugar supply would tell you nothing (linearly) about its obesity prevalence. As we will see in later chapters, this is roughly what happens within countries once we remove between-country differences.
With 37 countries, the vectors live in \(\mathbb{R}^{37}\). Why can't we visualize this space directly?
We can draw 2D or 3D spaces, but 37-dimensional space is beyond visual intuition. The scatter plot above is a different representation: each axis is a variable (sugar, obesity), and each dot is an observation (country). The vector geometry operates in observation space (\(\mathbb{R}^{37}\)), while the scatter plot operates in variable space (\(\mathbb{R}^2\)). The correlation is the same number either way.
Why do we center the vectors before computing r? What would happen if we didn't?
Without centering, the dot product would conflate the shared location of the data (both variables have positive means) with the actual direction of association. Two variables could have a high uncentered cosine simply because they are both far from the origin. Centering removes the overall level, so the angle reflects only how the deviations co-vary. Formally, centering projects both vectors onto the subspace orthogonal to the constant vector \(\mathbf{1}\).
In the widget above, dragging South Africa (the upper-right dot at 316 sugar, 46% obesity) changes r much more than dragging Ethiopia (78 sugar, 4% obesity). Why?
South Africa is far from the centroid on both variables, so it has high leverage. In the vector dot product, the terms \((x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})\) for South Africa are large because both deviations are large. Moving a high-leverage point changes the dot product substantially. Ethiopia is closer to the mean on sugar, so its leverage is lower. We will formalize leverage in a later chapter.
Deep dive: Why the normal equations are called "normal"

When you hear "normal equations" in regression, the word "normal" refers to geometry, not the normal distribution. In the context of least squares, the residual vector is normal (perpendicular) to the column space of X. The equations that enforce this perpendicularity are the normal equations:

$$\mathbf{X}^T(\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\hat{\boldsymbol\beta}) = \mathbf{0}$$

This says the residuals \(\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\hat{\boldsymbol\beta}\) are orthogonal to every column of \(\mathbf{X}\). We will see in Chapter 2 that this orthogonality condition is exactly what makes regression a perpendicular projection.

Deep dive: SXX, SXY, and why least squares formulas look the way they do

STAT 538 writes the least squares slope as \(\hat{\beta}_1 = SXY / SXX\), where:

$$SXY = \sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}), \qquad SXX = \sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2$$

Now look at the correlation formula: \(r = SXY / \sqrt{SXX \cdot SYY}\). The slope and correlation are related by \(\hat{\beta}_1 = r \cdot (s_y / s_x)\), where \(s_y\) and \(s_x\) are the standard deviations. The slope rescales the correlation to account for the different units of y and x. For our data:

$$\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.677 \times \frac{10.04}{106.47} = 0.0639 \text{ percentage points per kcal/day}$$

which is exactly the slope of the regression line in the scatter plot above.

We have a strong correlation: \(r = 0.677\). Countries with more sugar have more obesity. But correlation only measures the angle between two vectors. In Chapter 2, we fit a line through this cloud -- and that line turns out to be a projection.

2 Simple Regression as Projection STAT 538 M1

Core idea Regression projects the response vector \(\mathbf{y}\) onto the column space of \(\mathbf{X}\). The fitted values \(\hat{\mathbf{y}}\) are the shadow. The residuals \(\hat{\mathbf{e}}\) are the perpendicular gap. \(R^2 = \cos^2(\theta)\) where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\tilde{\mathbf{y}}\) and \(\hat{\mathbf{y}}\).

From correlation to regression

In Chapter 1 we measured the angle between two vectors. Now we do something different: we find the closest point in one subspace to a vector outside it. That closest point is the projection, and finding it is exactly what ordinary least squares does.

The SLR model says:

$$E(Y | X = x) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x$$

In matrix form, we collect the 481 observations (37 countries times 13 years, women's panel) into a response vector \(\mathbf{y}\) and a design matrix \(\mathbf{X}\) with columns for the intercept, sugar supply, and vegetable oil supply. Then we seek \(\hat{\boldsymbol\beta}\) that minimizes:

$$RSS = \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\hat{\boldsymbol\beta}\|^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2$$

The geometry: perpendicular projection

The columns of \(\mathbf{X}\) span a subspace (the "column space"). Every possible fitted value vector \(\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol\beta\) lives in this subspace. OLS finds the \(\hat{\boldsymbol\beta}\) that makes \(\hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{X}\hat{\boldsymbol\beta}\) as close to \(\mathbf{y}\) as possible. "As close as possible" means perpendicular drop.

The residual vector \(\hat{\mathbf{e}} = \mathbf{y} - \hat{\mathbf{y}}\) is orthogonal to every column of \(\mathbf{X}\). This is not an assumption -- it is a mathematical consequence of minimizing RSS. The normal equations enforce it:

$$\mathbf{X}^T \hat{\mathbf{e}} = \mathbf{0} \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad \hat{\mathbf{e}} \perp \text{col}(\mathbf{X})$$
Pythagorean theorem in high dimensions Because \(\hat{\mathbf{y}}\) and \(\hat{\mathbf{e}}\) are orthogonal, we get $$\|\tilde{\mathbf{y}}\|^2 = \|\hat{\mathbf{y}} - \bar{y}\mathbf{1}\|^2 + \|\hat{\mathbf{e}}\|^2$$ which is the sums-of-squares decomposition \(SST = SSReg + RSS\). This is literally the Pythagorean theorem applied in \(\mathbb{R}^{481}\).

R-squared is cosine squared

Divide both sides of the Pythagorean relation by \(SST\):

$$1 = \frac{SSReg}{SST} + \frac{RSS}{SST} = R^2 + (1 - R^2)$$

Now recall that the cosine of the angle \(\theta\) between \(\tilde{\mathbf{y}}\) (centered response) and \(\hat{\mathbf{y}} - \bar{y}\mathbf{1}\) (centered fitted values) satisfies:

$$\cos^2(\theta) = \frac{\|\hat{\mathbf{y}} - \bar{y}\mathbf{1}\|^2}{\|\tilde{\mathbf{y}}\|^2} = \frac{SSReg}{SST} = R^2$$

So \(R^2\) is the squared cosine of the angle between the centered response and its projection onto the model subspace. When \(R^2 = 0.49\), the angle is \(\arccos(\sqrt{0.49}) = \arccos(0.7) \approx 45.6^\circ\).

The pooled OLS: sugar looks enormous

When we project the obesity vector onto the column space spanned by an intercept, sugar supply, and vegetable oil supply using all 481 observations in the women's panel, we get the pooled OLS estimate. There are no country indicators, no year effects -- just one big regression mixing all between-country and within-country variation together.

import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

panel = pd.read_csv("data/derived/analysis_panel.csv")
women = panel[panel["sex"] == "Women"].copy()

# Scale sugar to 10-kcal units, oil to 100-kcal units (matching Table 1)
women["sugar_10kcal"] = women["primary_exposure_food_supply_kcal_capita_day"] / 10
women["oil_100kcal"] = women["vegetable_oils_comparison_food_supply_kcal_capita_day"] / 100

# Pooled OLS (Table 1, row 1)
fit = smf.ols("obesity_prevalence_pct ~ sugar_10kcal + oil_100kcal", data=women).fit()

print(f"beta_sugar = {fit.params['sugar_10kcal']:.3f}")
print(f"t_sugar    = {fit.tvalues['sugar_10kcal']:.2f}")
print(f"R-squared  = {fit.rsquared:.4f}")
print(f"N          = {len(women)}")

# Verify residuals are orthogonal to sugar
resid = fit.resid.values
sugar_col = women["sugar_10kcal"].values
print(f"\ndot(resid, sugar) = {np.dot(resid, sugar_col):.10f}")

# Verify R-squared = 1 - ||resid||^2 / ||centered_y||^2
y = women["obesity_prevalence_pct"].values
y_centered = y - y.mean()
r2_manual = 1 - np.dot(resid, resid) / np.dot(y_centered, y_centered)
print(f"R-squared (manual) = {r2_manual:.4f}")
beta_sugar = 0.630
t_sugar = 19.84
R-squared = 0.4878
N = 481

dot(resid, sugar) = 0.0000000000
R-squared (manual) = 0.4878

Two things to notice. First, the dot product of the residuals with sugar is exactly zero. This is not a coincidence -- it is the normal equations in action. The residual vector is orthogonal to the column space. Second, the manual \(R^2\) computation (\(1 - \|\hat{\mathbf{e}}\|^2 / \|\tilde{\mathbf{y}}\|^2\)) matches the model output exactly. Pythagorean theorem, verified numerically.

What do these numbers mean?

The coefficient \(\hat{\beta}_{sugar} = 0.630\) says: holding vegetable oil supply constant, each additional 10 kcal/day of sugar supply is associated with 0.63 percentage points higher obesity prevalence. That is the slope of the regression hyperplane in the sugar direction.

The t-statistic \(t = 19.84\) tests whether this slope is zero:

$$t = \frac{\hat{\beta}_1 - 0}{se(\hat{\beta}_1)} = \frac{0.630}{0.032} = 19.84$$

Under the null hypothesis \(H_0: \beta_1 = 0\), this t-statistic follows a \(t_{n-p}\) distribution with \(n - p = 481 - 3 = 478\) degrees of freedom. The p-value is vanishingly small. By every conventional standard, the sugar coefficient is overwhelmingly significant.

The paper number Table 1, row 1: Pooled OLS, \(\hat{\beta} = 0.630\), \(t = 19.84\), \(R^2 = 0.49\). This projection explains about half the variance. Sugar looks huge and overwhelming. But this regression mixes between-country variation (rich urbanized countries have more sugar and more obesity) with within-country variation (did obesity rise when sugar rose within the same country?). Every chapter from here forward will decompose and erode this number.
Paper connection "Pooled OLS, which mixes between-country and within-country variation, yields a strong sugar coefficient (\(t = 19.84\))." The paper proceeds to show that adding country fixed effects drops this to \(t = 0.25\). The projection onto a richer subspace (one that includes country indicators) yields a completely different answer. That is Chapter 5 of this textbook. For now, absorb the geometry: the number 0.630 is the length of a shadow, and the shadow will shrink when the screen changes.

Check your understanding

What hypothesis does the t-statistic \(t = 19.84\) test?
It tests \(H_0: \beta_{sugar} = 0\) vs. \(H_A: \beta_{sugar} \neq 0\). In words: "Does the mean of obesity change as a function of sugar supply, holding oil constant?" The answer here is an emphatic yes -- but only because pooled OLS mixes two kinds of variation. (Adapted from STAT 538 M1 Reading Quiz Q7 and Problem Solving Quiz Q2.)
We verified that \(\text{dot}(\hat{\mathbf{e}}, \mathbf{x}_{sugar}) = 0\). Why is this guaranteed?
The normal equations \(\mathbf{X}^T\hat{\mathbf{e}} = \mathbf{0}\) say that the residual is orthogonal to every column of \(\mathbf{X}\). Sugar is one of those columns, so the dot product is zero by construction. This is the defining property of a perpendicular projection: the "error" (distance from the subspace) points straight away from the subspace, not along it.
The residual standard error estimates which quantity?
It estimates \(\sigma\), the standard deviation of the error term \(e\). Specifically, \(s = \sqrt{RSS / (n - p)}\), where \(n - p\) are the degrees of freedom. The formula \(Var(e) = \sigma^2\) gives the variance; the residual SE is its square root. (Adapted from STAT 538 M1 Reading Quiz Q6.)
If \(R^2 = \cos^2(\theta)\) and our \(R^2 = 0.49\), what is the angle \(\theta\) between centered Y and its projection?
\(\theta = \arccos(\sqrt{0.49}) = \arccos(0.7) \approx 45.6^\circ\). The centered response and the centered fitted values make a 46-degree angle. The projection captures about half the "length" (variance) of Y. The residual carries the other half.
Why is the prediction interval for a new observation much wider than the confidence interval for the mean response?
The CI for \(E(Y|X=x^*)\) only accounts for uncertainty in the position of the regression plane. The prediction interval for a new \(Y_{new}\) must also account for the individual error \(e\), which adds an extra \(\sigma^2\) term. The formula is \(se(Y^*_{new} - \hat{y}^*) = s\sqrt{1 + 1/n + (x^* - \bar{x})^2/SXX}\) -- the "1 +" makes it much larger. (Adapted from STAT 538 M1 Reading Quiz Q9.)
In the projection widget above, what happens to \(R^2\) when you drag Y perpendicular to the X-line?
\(R^2 \to 0\). When Y is orthogonal to the column space, the projection \(\hat{Y}\) is zero (after centering). All of Y's length becomes residual. The model explains nothing. This is the geometric version of "no linear relationship."
Deep dive: The F-statistic and its relationship to t

The overall model F-statistic tests whether the model is better than intercept-only:

$$F = \frac{SSReg / p^*}{RSS / (n - p)} \sim F_{p^*, n-p}$$

where \(p^* = p - 1\) is the number of predictors (excluding intercept). In simple linear regression with one predictor, \(F = t^2\). With our pooled OLS, the sugar t-statistic is 19.84, and if we had a simple regression with sugar only, the F-statistic would be \(19.84^2 = 393.6\). The F-test asks "is the model better than no model?" while the t-test asks "is this particular slope nonzero?" In SLR they are the same question.

The ANOVA table decomposes this: \(SST = SSReg + RSS\). Each sum of squares is a squared vector length. The F-statistic is the ratio of per-dimension fit improvement to per-dimension residual. Geometrically, it asks: does projecting onto this extra dimension reduce the residual length enough to be worth the extra dimension?

Deep dive: Why the hat matrix matters

The projection is performed by the hat matrix:

$$\mathbf{H} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^T$$

So \(\hat{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{y}\). The diagonal entries \(h_{ii}\) are the leverages -- they measure how much influence observation \(i\) has on its own fitted value. If \(h_{ii} \approx 1\), that observation essentially determines its own fit. If \(h_{ii}\) is small, the fitted value is an average over many observations.

For our pooled OLS with 481 observations and 3 parameters, the average leverage is \(p/n = 3/481 = 0.006\). Countries like South Africa (high sugar, high obesity) or The Gambia (very high sugar, moderate obesity) have higher leverage because they sit far from the centroid of X.

The residual variance is not constant across observations: \(Var(\hat{e}_i) = \sigma^2(1 - h_{ii})\). High-leverage points have smaller residual variance because the regression line is pulled toward them. This is why standardized residuals \(r_i = \hat{e}_i / (s\sqrt{1 - h_{ii}})\) divide by the leverage-adjusted standard deviation.

Deep dive: Connecting R-squared in Chapters 1 and 2

In Chapter 1 we computed \(r = 0.677\) (bivariate correlation between sugar and obesity, 37 country means). In Chapter 2 we computed \(R^2 = 0.488\) (pooled OLS with sugar + oil, 481 panel observations). These are related but not identical:

For a bivariate regression (one predictor, no controls), \(R^2 = r^2\). Indeed, \(0.677^2 = 0.458\). But our Chapter 2 regression includes vegetable oil as a control and uses 481 panel observations rather than 37 country means. With two predictors, \(R^2\) measures the squared cosine of the angle between centered Y and its projection onto the column space of both predictors. It is higher than \(r^2\) for sugar alone because oil adds explanatory power.

The key distinction: \(r\) in Chapter 1 measured alignment between two specific variables. \(R^2\) in Chapter 2 measures how much of Y's variance the entire model subspace captures. Both are cosines, but of different angles in different spaces.

The pooled OLS projects obesity onto a 3-dimensional subspace (intercept + sugar + oil) and finds a strong sugar signal: \(\hat{\beta} = 0.630\), \(t = 19.84\). But that subspace does not account for the fact that countries differ in ways that correlate with both sugar and obesity. Chapter 5 will expand the subspace to 39 dimensions by adding 36 country dummies. The projection onto that richer space will tell a very different story.

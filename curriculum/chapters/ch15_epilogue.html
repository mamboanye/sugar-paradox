15 Cross-Country Change and Initial Conditions

What you learn If food supply does not predict which countries gained more obesity, what does? A cross-country change regression (\(N = 37\), one observation per country) tests this directly. The only significant predictor at the 5% level is initial (2010) obesity: \(t = 3.45\), \(R^2 = 0.41\). Countries that started fatter got fatter faster. This is divergence, not convergence.

The question

Chapters 5 through 14 established that food supply changes do not predict obesity changes within countries. Six specifications, ten food groups, two health outcomes, all null. The natural follow-up: if not food, then what?

To answer this, we switch from panel regression to a simpler design. Collapse each country into a single observation: compute the 2010-to-2020 change in obesity, and regress it on corresponding changes in sugar supply, vegetable oil supply, GDP, urbanization, and the 2010 starting level of obesity. This is ordinary multiple regression -- back to the framework from Chapter 3 -- but now with \(N = 37\) country-level observations instead of 481 panel rows.

An honest look at the numbers The paper's abstract says initial obesity is "the only predictor." At a strict \(\alpha = 0.05\) threshold, that is true: only init_obesity passes (\(p = 0.002\)). But urbanization change is at \(t = 1.73\) (\(p = 0.094\)) and vegetable oil change is at \(t = -1.84\) (\(p = 0.075\)). Both are marginally significant at the 10% level. With only 37 observations and 5 predictors (31 residual df), these marginal results carry real uncertainty. The paper's body text acknowledges these marginals -- "Urbanization change is marginally positive (\(t = 1.73\), \(p = 0.09\)). Vegetable oil change is marginally negative (\(t = -1.84\), \(p = 0.08\))" -- but the abstract streamlines to "the only predictor." This is a judgment call, not a fabrication. A more cautious phrasing might be: "the only predictor significant at the 5% level, with urbanization and oil change marginally suggestive." The core finding -- that food supply changes contribute nothing, while structural initial conditions drive divergence -- holds regardless. But notice how framing choices shape emphasis, even in honest work.

What the scatter tells you

Tab A shows a clear positive relationship. South Africa (\(\text{init} = 40.6\%\)) gained 5.6 pp. Madagascar (\(\text{init} = 2.2\%\)) gained 1.6 pp. The gap widened. Countries further along the obesity transition gained more, not less. The bivariate \(r = 0.51\) is significant at \(p = 0.001\).

Tab B shows nothing. Gambia's sugar supply increased by 120 kcal/day and its obesity rose 6.8 pp. Eswatini's sugar supply fell by 107 kcal/day and its obesity rose 8.8 pp. Gains and losses in sugar supply have no relationship with gains in obesity (\(r = -0.19\), \(p = 0.25\)).

This is the final piece of the puzzle. The cross-sectional sugar-obesity correlation exists because high-sugar countries are the same high-obesity countries, and these countries gain obesity faster than low-obesity countries. The gap widens year by year, strengthening the cross-sectional association at every snapshot. But the widening is driven by where countries started, not by what changed in their food supply.

Divergence, not convergence

All 37 countries gained obesity over 2010-2020. Every single one. The mean gain was 4.9 percentage points. The range was 1.4 to 9.4. Countries do not converge to a common obesity level; they diverge from their starting positions. The initial-conditions coefficient (\(\hat{\beta} = 0.12\)) means that a country starting 10 percentage points higher gained roughly 1.2 pp more over the decade.

This divergence pattern is consistent with structural determinants: built environment, food retail infrastructure, sedentary employment, and other features of economic development that correlate with initial obesity levels. These conditions are self-reinforcing. Countries further along the economic transition have environments that promote weight gain, and those environments accumulate.

The code that produces Table 3

import statsmodels.formula.api as smf

# One observation per country: 2010-to-2020 changes
fit = smf.ols(
    "d_obesity ~ d_urban + d_sugar + d_oil + d_gdp + init_obesity",
    data=change_df
).fit()

print(f"R2 = {fit.rsquared:.3f}")
for var in ["d_urban", "d_sugar", "d_oil", "d_gdp", "init_obesity"]:
    b = fit.params[var]
    t = fit.tvalues[var]
    p = fit.pvalues[var]
    print(f"  {var}: beta={b:.4f}, t={t:.2f}, p={p:.4f}")

Quiz yourself

Why does the cross-sectional sugar-obesity correlation get stronger over time if sugar supply has no within-country effect?
Because the countries that already had high obesity (and happen to have high sugar supply) gain more obesity than countries that started low. The gap widens, strengthening the snapshot correlation at any point in time. It is divergence driven by initial conditions, not a dietary effect strengthening over time.
This is an \(N = 37\) regression with 5 predictors (31 residual df). What should you worry about?
Power. With only 37 observations and 31 residual df, marginal effects (like urbanization at \(t = 1.73\)) carry wide confidence intervals. The regression can confidently detect a strong predictor like initial obesity, but cannot distinguish marginal predictors from noise. Multicollinearity between the change variables is also a concern -- countries that urbanized faster may also have experienced faster GDP growth.
Urbanization change has \(t = 1.73\) (\(p = 0.094\)). Vegetable oil change has \(t = -1.84\) (\(p = 0.075\)). The paper calls initial obesity "the only predictor." Is that overclaiming?
At a strict 5% threshold, it is accurate. But the two marginal results are close enough that a different sample or specification might flip them across the threshold. The paper's body text reports these marginals transparently. A fairer summary might be "the only predictor at conventional significance, with urbanization suggestive." Whether this constitutes overclaiming depends on how strictly you read "the only predictor" -- it is the kind of framing choice that amplifies a result without technically misrepresenting it. Good practice: always report the full table, as the paper does.
Deep dive: Why initial conditions create divergence

The coefficient on initial obesity is 0.12. This is less than 1, so countries are not diverging explosively -- it is not the case that obesity doubles proportionally. Rather, the growth rate of obesity is slightly higher in countries that start higher. This is consistent with a "Matthew effect" in public health: countries with more obesogenic environments gain weight faster because those environments compound. Built infrastructure, processed food retail, sedentary employment -- these structural features correlate with current obesity levels and accelerate further gain.

Mathematically, if \(y_{2020} = y_{2010} + \alpha + \beta \cdot y_{2010}\), then \(\Delta y = \alpha + \beta \cdot y_{2010}\). With \(\beta = 0.12 > 0\), the change is larger for higher starting values. This is a divergence process. If \(\beta\) were negative, it would indicate convergence (lagging countries catching up). The data clearly reject convergence.

The paper is the curriculum

This is not a textbook that uses a paper as an illustration. The paper is the curriculum. Every concept you learned exists because the data required it. Every number you computed appears in a published analysis. The progression from Chapter 1 to Chapter 15 is also the progression from a naive cross-sectional analysis to a full identification argument.

The paradox -- a strong cross-sectional correlation that vanishes within countries -- is not a curiosity. It is the central pedagogical device. You cannot understand why the correlation vanishes without understanding fixed effects, variance decomposition, serial correlation, and detrending. You cannot evaluate whether the null is real without bootstrap inference, positive controls, and placebo tests. You cannot explain what drives cross-country differences without the change regression in Chapter 15.

The curriculum is the paper.

You began this textbook watching a correlation of 0.677 and thinking sugar might cause obesity. You end it knowing that the correlation reflects development gradients, that within-country variation is null across every specification, that 99.4% of obesity variance is absorbed by country and year effects, and that the only predictor of which countries get fatter faster is how fat they already were.

The question was never about sugar.

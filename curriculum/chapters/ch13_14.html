13 The Food-Group Cascade

STAT 538 concepts: M8 (Model Selection) / M5 (Multiple Testing) If the null were specific to sugar, you could blame measurement error or a peculiarity of the sugar supply variable. But the null applies to all 10 FAOSTAT food groups. The cascade pattern -- many cross-sectional positives, fewer one-way FE positives, zero TWFE survivors, zero first-difference survivors -- is the signature of trend domination, not of one bad variable.

The model selection question

Model selection (M8) asks: which variables belong in the model? The standard tools -- AIC, BIC, adjusted \(R^2\), \(C_p\) -- rank models by fit-complexity tradeoff. But those criteria assume the candidate variables have genuine predictive power to evaluate. What happens when none of them do?

The food-group cascade is a model selection result in the negative: under the most demanding specifications, no food group enters. AIC and BIC would agree. The interesting question is not which food group to select, but why so many appear to matter under weaker specifications.

Multiple testing and the cascade

Testing 10 food groups under 4 specifications is 40 hypothesis tests. Under a strict Bonferroni correction (\(\alpha = 0.05 / 40 = 0.00125\)), even cross-sectionally only sugar (\(p < 0.001\)) and meat (\(p < 0.001\)) survive. But the cascade makes a stronger argument than any correction: every food group tells the same story of progressive collapse.

What the cascade means for model selection

In a cross-sectional regression, a model selection algorithm could legitimately choose sugar, meat, milk, starchy roots, pulses, or animal fats as predictors. Six of ten candidates look promising. Forward selection might pick sugar first (\(r = 0.68\)), then meat (\(r = 0.65\)), then milk (\(r = 0.50\)).

But these are not independent signals. They are all proxies for the same underlying development gradient. Rich, urbanized countries have more of everything -- more sugar, more meat, more milk, more obesity. Model selection on cross-sectional data selects for variables that track development, not variables that cause obesity.

Under one-way FE, the candidate pool shrinks to meat (\(t = 3.05\)) and vegetables (\(t = 4.13\)). Notably, vegetables were not cross-sectionally significant (\(r = 0.07\)), while several cross-sectionally strong variables (sugar, milk, animal fats) collapse. The between-country and within-country signals disagree about which food groups matter.

Under TWFE and first differences, the candidate pool is empty. Zero of ten. No food group clears the bar when shared trends are removed.

Deep dive: Why vegetables appear under one-way FE but not cross-sectionally

Vegetables have a cross-sectional \(r = 0.07\) (essentially zero) but a one-way FE \(t = 4.13\). How can a variable with no between-country signal have a strong within-country signal?

One possibility: within-country vegetable supply changes are correlated with the obesity trend for reasons unrelated to diet (both track urbanization and supply chain development). But this within-country signal is still confounded by the shared time trend. Once year FE absorb that trend (TWFE), vegetables collapse to \(t = -0.38\). The one-way FE signal was co-trending, just like the cross-sectional signals for other food groups.

This is a clean illustration of why model selection results depend entirely on the specification. The "best" predictor under one-way FE (vegetables) is not even a candidate cross-sectionally, and neither survives TWFE.

Deep dive: Model selection with correlated candidates

Standard model selection assumes that adding a variable either helps or doesn't. With food groups, the candidates are correlated -- countries that eat more of one food tend to eat more of others (the development gradient). Multicollinearity (M7) means that including multiple food groups simultaneously would inflate standard errors, but that is not the main problem here.

The main problem is that all food groups share the same confounding structure: trending over time and correlated with development cross-sectionally. No amount of selection among confounded candidates produces an unconfounded model. The cascade shows this: the entire basket collapses, not just the weakest members.

Quiz yourself

If you ran BIC-based model selection on the TWFE specification with all 10 food groups as candidates, what model would BIC select?
The null model -- no food groups included. Every food group adds a parameter without reducing SSE enough to overcome BIC's \(\log(n)\) penalty. When zero of ten candidates is significant at even the 5% level, BIC (which penalizes more harshly than 5% thresholds) would exclude all of them.
Meat has the strongest one-way FE signal (\(t = 3.05\)). Could you publish a paper claiming that meat consumption drives obesity in SSA based on this?
You could, and it would pass peer review at many journals. The one-way FE controls for all time-invariant country characteristics. But the cascade shows that this result is an artifact of the shared time trend: under TWFE, meat drops to \(t = -0.50\). The same trap that catches sugar catches meat. Any food variable that trends upward in the same countries where obesity trends upward will appear significant under one-way FE.
Pulses and starchy roots have negative cross-sectional correlations (\(r = -0.41\) and \(r = -0.36\)). What does that mean?
Countries with higher pulse and starchy root supply tend to have lower obesity. These are staple foods of lower-income, more rural countries. The negative correlation reflects the same development gradient as the positive correlations for sugar and meat, just from the other end: poor countries eat more pulses and starchy roots, and poor countries have less obesity. Once you control for the development gradient (via FE), both the positive and negative associations collapse.

Every food group tells the same story: the cross-sectional signal is a development artifact, not a dietary effect. But the cascade has one near-exception. Chapter 14 examines what happens when we switch from obesity to diabetes, where the residual variance is larger and the TWFE sugar coefficient creeps toward significance.

14 The Diabetes Borderline Case

STAT 538 concept: M7 (Diagnostics for MLR -- sensitivity analysis) Diabetes has more residual variance after fixed effects (4.4% vs. 0.6% for obesity). That means there is more room for a food variable to find something. The TWFE sugar-diabetes coefficient is borderline (\(t = 1.92\), \(p = 0.055\)). But detrending kills it (\(t = 0.26\)). The borderline signal is residual co-trending exploiting the extra room.

Why diabetes is different from obesity

Obesity trends in SSA are remarkably uniform: nearly linear, with entity and year fixed effects absorbing 99.4% of variance. Diabetes trends are noisier. Entity and year FE absorb only 95.6%, leaving 4.4% residual -- 7.7 times more room than obesity's 0.6%.

More room means more opportunity for any trending variable to pick up a signal in the residual. And sugar supply trends over time. The question is whether the TWFE sugar-diabetes result reflects a real dietary effect or is just residual co-trending with more room to operate.

The sensitivity analysis argument (M7)

M7 teaches that diagnostics assess whether your conclusions are robust to violations of assumptions. The key diagnostic question here: does the result survive alternative specifications that are even more demanding about removing trends?

TWFE removes a common year effect (one intercept per year, shared across all countries). But if individual countries have different time trends -- some steepening, some flattening -- TWFE does not remove those. Detrending removes each country's individual linear trajectory. It is strictly more demanding.

If the TWFE signal is a real dietary effect, detrending should preserve it (the signal exists in deviations from trend). If the TWFE signal is residual co-trending, detrending should kill it (removing the trend removes the signal).

The numbers

Diabetes TWFE: \(\hat{\beta} = 0.084\), \(t = 1.92\), \(p = 0.055\). This is the closest any specification gets to a conventional threshold in the entire analysis. A hostile reviewer could call this borderline significant.

But three more demanding specifications all yield null:

  • Detrended: \(t = 0.26\), \(p = 0.80\) -- dead null
  • First differences: \(r = 0.04\), \(p = 0.41\) -- dead null
  • Long differences: \(r = 0.24\), \(p = 0.15\) -- null

The Mundlak CRE decomposition tells the same story. Within-country: \(t = 1.99\) (borderline). Between-country: \(t = 2.88\) (significant). The between-country signal is stronger, just as with obesity. The within-country signal is just borderline enough to create doubt, but detrending resolves the doubt.

The code

Deep dive: Why TWFE does not fully remove trends

Two-way FE include one dummy per year, shared across all countries. This removes the average year effect. If all countries' diabetes trajectories were parallel (same slope, different levels), TWFE would remove the trend completely.

But diabetes trends vary across countries. Some countries' diabetes is accelerating, others are decelerating. TWFE removes the mean trajectory but leaves country-specific deviations from that mean. If sugar supply also has country-specific trends, the residual sugar trend can correlate with the residual diabetes trend -- this is what produces the borderline \(t = 1.92\).

Detrending removes each country's individual linear trend. After detrending, only deviations from the country-specific trajectory remain. The fact that \(t\) drops from 1.92 to 0.26 means the TWFE signal lived entirely in the country-specific trend components, not in deviations from trend.

Deep dive: The 7.7x residual room and statistical power

With 0.6% residual variance (obesity), the standard deviation of residuals is tiny. Any food supply variable's coefficient is divided by this tiny residual variation, but the numerator (covariance of food with residual obesity) is also tiny because there is nothing systematic left to predict. The result: \(t\)-statistics near zero.

With 4.4% residual variance (diabetes), the residual is \(\sqrt{4.4 / 0.6} \approx 2.7\) times larger in standard deviation. There is more room for both real signals and spurious co-trending to register. The borderline TWFE result for diabetes is not evidence of a real signal -- it is evidence of more noise, and within that noise, residual trends can briefly look like signal.

Quiz yourself

A reviewer argues: "The TWFE sugar-diabetes coefficient has \(t = 1.92\) (\(p = 0.055\)). With a slightly larger sample, this would be significant. How do you respond?"
The \(p\)-value is not the issue. The issue is that the result does not survive more demanding specifications. Detrending gives \(t = 0.26\), first differences give \(r = 0.04\). If the signal were real, it would persist when trends are removed more aggressively. It does not. A larger sample would give more precise estimates of zero under detrending and FD, not rescue the TWFE result.
Why is the variance decomposition comparison (99.4% vs. 95.6%) the key to understanding the diabetes borderline case?
Because it explains why diabetes can produce a borderline result that obesity cannot. With obesity, there is only 0.6% of variance left after FE -- too little room for any food variable. With diabetes, there is 4.4% -- enough room for residual co-trending to register as borderline significant. The difference in residual space, not the difference in biology, explains the difference in results. This is a pure diagnostics (M7) argument: the borderline result is an artifact of the variance structure, not evidence of a dietary effect.
The CRE within-country diabetes coefficient is \(t = 1.99\), which is just past the conventional threshold. Does this change the conclusion?
No. The CRE within-country coefficient is numerically equivalent to the TWFE coefficient under standard assumptions. Both are borderline. The question is not "is it past 1.96?" but "does it survive detrending?" It does not. The \(t = 1.99\) and \(t = 1.92\) are measuring the same signal from two angles, and both are killed by detrending (\(t = 0.26\)).

The diabetes borderline case is the most vulnerable point in the paper's argument. Understanding it requires the full M7 toolkit: sensitivity analysis, specification testing, and variance decomposition.

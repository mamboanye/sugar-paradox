5 Fixed Effects as Expanding the Subspace M4 / M5

What you learn Adding 36 country dummy variables expands the column space of \(X\) from 3 dimensions (intercept, sugar, oil) to 39. The projection of \(Y\) gets much closer to \(Y\) itself -- \(R^2\) jumps from 0.49 to 0.96. But sugar's unique contribution -- the improvement from adding sugar to a subspace that already contains country dummies -- shrinks to nearly nothing. The sugar \(t\)-statistic drops from 19.84 to 0.25.

The pooled picture

Start with pooled OLS: regress obesity on sugar and oil, ignoring which country each observation belongs to. The model sees 481 rows (37 countries, 13 years, women) as interchangeable data points. Sugar's coefficient is 0.630 with \(t = 19.84\). That is enormous. One more unit of sugar-supply-per-10-kcal predicts 0.63 percentage points more obesity.

But this model is wrong. It stacks Mauritius (21% obesity, 527 kcal sugar) alongside Niger (3% obesity, 29 kcal sugar) and treats the gap between them the same as the gap between Niger-2010 and Niger-2015. The between-country variation swamps everything. The sugar coefficient is identifying development gradients, not dietary effects.

What happened geometrically?

In the pooled model, the column space of \(X\) is 3-dimensional: an intercept, a sugar column, and an oil column. The hat matrix projects \(Y\) onto this thin subspace. Sugar does most of the work because it correlates with the dominant direction of variation in \(Y\) (between-country level differences).

Adding 36 country dummies expands \(X\) to 39 columns. Now each country gets its own intercept. The projection of \(Y\) onto this richer subspace lands much closer to \(Y\) -- \(R^2\) jumps to 0.96. But sugar's marginal contribution, on top of country dummies that already explain 95.7%, is negligible. The partial \(F\)-statistic for sugar is \(0.25^2 = 0.06\), far below 1.

The F-test connection (M5) Adding sugar to the country FE model is an F-test comparing two projections. The small model (country dummies + oil) projects \(Y\) onto a 38-dimensional subspace. The big model (country dummies + oil + sugar) projects onto 39 dimensions. The F-test asks: does the extra dimension reduce RSS enough to justify itself? Here, \(F = t^2 = 0.25^2 = 0.06\). It does not.

Check your understanding

Why does \(R^2\) increase from 0.49 to 0.96 when we add country dummies, even though the sugar coefficient collapses?
\(R^2\) measures total explained variance, not any single predictor's contribution. The 36 country dummies alone explain 95.7% of obesity variance by absorbing level differences between countries. Sugar's marginal contribution is tiny on top of that. Adding predictors can only increase \(R^2\) -- this is a mechanical property of the projection getting closer to \(Y\) in a higher-dimensional subspace.
A colleague says: "The FE model has low power because sugar does not vary much within countries." How do you evaluate this?
This is a legitimate concern -- and the paper addresses it. The positive control is GDP: it has a cross-sectional correlation of 0.73 with obesity, yet under detrending the sign reverses (t = -4.12, significantly negative). The method has power to detect within-country relationships when they exist. Sugar shows nothing because there is nothing to detect, not because the method is blind.
In the LOO explorer, what does it mean that the range is 0.42 to 0.63?
It means no single country's removal pushes the partial r below 0.40 or changes its sign. The cross-sectional association is robust to influential observations. But robustness of a cross-sectional estimate does not validate it as a causal claim. The entire robust association is between-country, and the Mundlak decomposition confirms it: between-country sugar t = 3.46, within-country sugar t = -0.66.
Deep dive: the Frisch-Waugh-Lovell theorem

The FE sugar coefficient is numerically identical to this three-step procedure: (1) regress sugar on the country dummies and oil, save the residuals; (2) regress obesity on the country dummies and oil, save the residuals; (3) regress the obesity residuals on the sugar residuals. The slope in step (3) is the FE sugar coefficient.

$$\hat{\beta}_{\text{sugar}}^{\text{FE}} = \frac{(M_Z \, x_{\text{sugar}})' \, (M_Z \, y)}{(M_Z \, x_{\text{sugar}})' \, (M_Z \, x_{\text{sugar}})}$$

where \(M_Z = I - Z(Z'Z)^{-1}Z'\) projects off the country dummies and oil. The numerator is the covariance of within-country sugar deviations and within-country obesity deviations. It is near zero. Sugar and obesity do not co-move within countries.

Deep dive: what are the country dummies doing?

In M4-V2, you learned that dummy variables create shifted intercepts. Here, \(C(\text{iso3})\) creates 36 indicator columns (one per country, minus one baseline). Each country gets its own intercept: obesity_AGO = 5.2 + 0.035 * sugar + ..., obesity_ZAF = 38.1 + 0.035 * sugar + .... The intercept absorbs each country's average obesity level. Sugar's coefficient now measures: "within a given country, if sugar supply were 10 kcal higher in one year than another, how much higher would obesity be?" The answer: 0.035 percentage points, with \(t = 0.25\). Indistinguishable from zero.

The country dummies absorbed the level differences between countries. But all 37 countries share an upward trend in obesity -- they are all getting fatter over time. Chapter 6 adds year dummies to absorb that shared trend, pushing \(R^2\) to 99.4% and leaving just 0.6% for any food variable to explain.

6 Two-Way FE and Variance Decomposition M4 / M5

What you learn Year fixed effects absorb the shared upward trend in obesity common to all countries. After adding 12 year dummies on top of 36 country dummies, the model explains 99.4% of obesity variance. The residual is 0.6%. Any food variable must compete in that sliver. Sugar's \(t\) drops from 0.25 to -0.64.

Why year fixed effects?

Country FE removed between-country differences. But obesity is rising in nearly every country over 2010-2022. This shared upward trend is captured by the country-specific intercepts only partially -- it is a common time effect. Year fixed effects give each year its own intercept, absorbing the portion of the trend shared across all countries.

After removing both between-country levels and the common time trend, the only variation left is country-specific deviations from their own trend. This is a very small residual. Country-year cells that are slightly above or below what you would predict from the country's level and the year's common shift. Sugar must explain this deviation, and it cannot.

The geometry

Think of the 481 obesity observations as a vector in \(\mathbb{R}^{481}\). The intercept-only model projects this vector onto a 1-dimensional subspace (the constant vector). Country dummies expand this to 37 dimensions. Year dummies expand further to 49 dimensions (37 countries + 12 year indicators). At 49 dimensions, the projection captures 99.4% of the length of \(Y\). The residual vector -- the part orthogonal to this 49-dimensional subspace -- has only 0.6% of the original variance.

Adding sugar expands the subspace by one more dimension, to 50. But this extra dimension does not shorten the residual. The improvement in fit is statistically zero. That is what \(t = -0.64\) means.

The key insight The null result is not about sugar specifically. Entity and year FE absorb 99.4% of obesity variance because obesity trajectories in SSA are well-described by a country-specific level plus a common time trend. The residual 0.6% is dominated by measurement error and short-run noise. No food supply variable -- not sugar, not oil, not meat, not any of the 10 FAOSTAT food groups -- predicts this residual. The signal was never in the food. It was in the trends.

Comparing nested models: the F-test view

Each step in the variance decomposition is a nested model comparison, exactly as in M5. We can write the F-test for each:

# F-test: does sugar improve fit beyond country + year FE?
fit_no_sugar = smf.ols(
    "obesity_prevalence_pct ~ oil_100kcal + C(iso3) + C(year)", data=women
).fit()
fit_with_sugar = smf.ols(
    "obesity_prevalence_pct ~ sugar_10kcal + oil_100kcal + C(iso3) + C(year)",
    data=women
).fit()

RSS_reduced = fit_no_sugar.ssr
RSS_full    = fit_with_sugar.ssr
n, p        = fit_with_sugar.nobs, fit_with_sugar.df_model + 1
F_sugar     = ((RSS_reduced - RSS_full) / 1) / (RSS_full / (n - p))
# F = t^2 when testing a single predictor
F = 0.41   (non-clustered t\(^2\); with clustering, t = -0.64, giving t\(^2\) = 0.41)

Check your understanding

If 99.4% of variance is absorbed, does that mean the model predicts obesity well for new countries?
No. The high \(R^2\) comes from country-specific intercepts and year effects, which are fitted to the observed countries and years. For a new country not in the panel, you would not have a country intercept. The 99.4% is in-sample fit, not predictive accuracy for out-of-sample countries. This connects to M6's distinction between in-sample fit and genuine prediction.
The residual is 0.6%. Could a better food variable find signal in that 0.6%?
In principle, yes. But the paper tests all 10 FAOSTAT food groups, and none predicts within-country obesity change. The 0.6% residual is dominated by measurement noise and short-run deviations. A positive control confirms the method works: GDP has a detrended t = -4.12, proving the approach has power when there is signal. The 0.6% simply does not contain dietary signal at the resolution of national food supply data.
Why do we report the sugar coefficient from TWFE rather than stopping at country FE?
Country FE removes between-country levels but leaves the shared time trend. If sugar supply and obesity both trend upward over 2010-2022 (which they do in many countries), one-way FE can still find a spurious positive association through co-trending. Year FE removes this shared trend. The soybean oil case study (Chapter 12 of this textbook) demonstrates this: soybean oil is significant under country FE (t = 2.09) but null under TWFE (t = 0.70). The shared trend was doing the work, not the food variable.
Deep dive: the diabetes borderline case

Diabetes has a residual variance of 4.4% after FE, compared to obesity's 0.6%. That is 7.7 times more room for a food variable to find something. And indeed, the TWFE sugar-diabetes coefficient is borderline: \(t = 1.92\), \(p = 0.055\). But entity-specific detrending kills it: \(t = 0.26\). The borderline signal is residual co-trending exploiting the extra room. More residual variance does not mean more causal signal -- it means more opportunity for spurious correlation.

Deep dive: is OLS still BLUE in this model?

Gauss-Markov (M4) guarantees OLS is the Best Linear Unbiased Estimator when errors are uncorrelated with equal variance. Panel data violates both conditions: errors within the same country are serially correlated (AR(1) of TWFE residuals averages 0.90), and error variance may differ across countries. Clustered standard errors correct the inference (t-statistics, p-values) without changing the point estimate. The SE inflation is 2.6-2.9x, meaning non-clustered standard errors overstate precision by nearly a factor of 3. A coefficient with non-clustered \(t = 1.9\) has clustered \(t = 0.65\). This matters: published studies that do not cluster can report significant results where none exist.

Two chapters, one story. The pooled regression found \(t = 19.84\) because it confused "countries that eat more sugar are fatter" with "countries that started eating more sugar got fatter." Country FE removed the levels. Year FE removed the shared trend. What remains is 0.6% residual noise with no food-supply signal. The next chapter examines what happens to the standard errors in this setting -- and why it matters for published results that did not cluster.

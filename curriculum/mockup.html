Quiz yourself

Why does R-squared increase from 0.49 to 0.96 when we add country dummies, even though the sugar coefficient collapses?
R-squared measures total explained variance, not any single predictor's contribution. The 36 country dummies explain 95.7% of obesity variance by absorbing level differences. Sugar's marginal contribution is tiny on top of that. Adding predictors always increases R-squared.
In the LOO explorer above, what does it mean that the range is 0.42 to 0.63? Is the result robust?
Yes. No single country's removal pushes the partial r below 0.40 or changes its sign. The cross-sectional association is stable across all 37 countries. The paradox isn't that the cross-sectional evidence is fragile (it isn't). The paradox is that this stable cross-sectional evidence tells you nothing about within-country effects.
Deep dive: The Frisch-Waugh-Lovell theorem

The FE sugar coefficient is identical to: (1) regress sugar on country dummies + oil, save residuals; (2) regress obesity on country dummies + oil, save residuals; (3) regress the obesity residuals on the sugar residuals. The slope of step 3 IS the FE coefficient.

$$\hat{\beta}_{\text{sugar}}^{\text{FE}} = \frac{(M_Z \, x_{\text{sugar}})' \, (M_Z \, y)}{(M_Z \, x_{\text{sugar}})' \, (M_Z \, x_{\text{sugar}})}$$

where \(M_Z = I - Z(Z'Z)^{-1}Z'\) projects off the country dummies and oil. The numerator is the covariance of within-country sugar and within-country obesity. It is near zero.

The country dummies absorbed level differences. But all 37 countries share an upward obesity trend. Chapter 6 adds year dummies to absorb that trend too, pushing R\(^2\) to 99.4% and leaving just 0.6% for any food variable.
